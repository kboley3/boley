Faint Galaxy Detection

At ∼ 1015 M⊙, galaxy clusters are the most massive gravitationally bound objects in the universe, which produce gravitational lenses. Using gravitational lensing, we are able to detect spatially resolved galaxies at a redshift ∼10, only 400 Myr after the Big Bang. SPT0615-JD is a candidate z∼10 (z=9.9± 0.6) galaxy that is spatially resolved in Hubble Space Telescope (HST) imaging of galaxy cluster SPT- CLJ0254-5857. Three independent lens models predict two counter images for SPT0615-JD. One of these counter images is predicted to be below the existing detection limits of HST. The other counter image is calculated to be above the existing detection limits of HST and of similar magnitude of the previously detected image; however, conventional detections methods have yet to discover it. Given the consistency of the lensing models, this may be an indication that galaxy is not at z∼10. Attempting to resolve this discrepancy, we apply a maximum likelihood detection method, which is capable of increasing the detection sensitivity for extended objects. We find a significant detection of SPT0615- JD in the F140W band. We also identified potential counter images of significance >3σ. The flux emitted by the counter image candidates and the significance were evaluated at multiple wavelengths. We find that the SED of these candidates are inconsistent with the SED of the original candidate z∼10 galaxy. Assuming the cluster lens models are accurate, our results suggest that SPT0615-JD is not a z∼10 galaxy, but rather a z∼2 galaxy, the second most likely photometric redshift.
